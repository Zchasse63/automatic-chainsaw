### 1.1 Chris Hinshaw ‚Äî Aerobic Capacity and the Endurance Engine

Chris Hinshaw is the founder of the Aerobic Capacity programming system and has coached over 35 CrossFit Games champions. Hinshaw's central thesis ‚Äî which he calls "building the engine" ‚Äî is that an athlete's aerobic base is the mechanical foundation that allows all other physical capacities to express themselves at higher volumes and for longer durations.

**Core Philosophy: The Aerobic Engine**

Hinshaw's philosophy rests on several key principles drawn from his coaching experience and endurance sport background (Source: *PMPC Podcast #004, Chris Hinshaw ‚Äì Aerobic Capacity Founder*, 2024; *Best Hour of Their Day* episodes 561-562; *Jedburgh Podcast*):

1. **Fast-twitch fibers create fatigue; slow-twitch fibers clear it.** During high-intensity work, fast-twitch muscle fibers produce lactate and other fatigue-inducing byproducts. Slow-twitch fibers consume that lactate as fuel. If an athlete only trains high intensity, they become excellent at *producing* lactate and working under its burden, but they never develop the capacity to *clear* it. This is the bottleneck ‚Äî and the aerobic engine is the solution.

2. **If you have a 50/50 blend of fast-twitch and slow-twitch fibers and only train fast-twitch, you are leaving 50% of your available capacity passive.** Slow-twitch fibers require volume and time under tension to adapt. Short, explosive training sessions do not provide the overload stimulus these fibers need. This is especially critical for Hyrox, where the race lasts 60‚Äì90+ minutes and demands sustained output across diverse movement patterns.

3. **Every movement is unique, and every intensity within that movement is unique.** Hinshaw insists that athletes train across a "variety of intensities." Speed alone does not develop the full spectrum of the aerobic system. You must train the low end (base building, lactate clearance, capillarization), the moderate range (tempo and threshold work), and the high end (VO2max intervals) to build a complete engine.

4. **Pacing is the ultimate competitive advantage.** Hinshaw was one of the earliest advocates of pacing strategy in functional fitness. He teaches that efficiently and effectively consuming available energy through controlled pacing maximizes total performance. In Hyrox, where athletes must sustain effort across 8 km of running interspersed with 8 functional stations, pacing is not optional ‚Äî it is the difference between a competitive finish and a collapse.

5. **Long-term cellular adaptation beats short-term intensity spikes.** While high-intensity protocols produce quick results, Hinshaw warns that these adaptations are "non-sustainable" and "riddled with risk." The real gains come from cellular-level adaptation: increased mitochondrial density, capillarization, improved fat oxidation, and enhanced stroke volume. These take months to develop but last far longer and provide a far higher ceiling.

## 5. Concurrent Training Research

### 5.1 The Interference Effect: What the Science Actually Says

The "interference effect" was first documented by Hickson (1980), who showed that untrained men doing both strength and endurance training simultaneously had reduced strength gains compared to those doing strength training alone. This spawned decades of concern that "cardio kills gains."
